# Facilitation Patterns

Use this reference when building host rules, release timing, and debriefs.

## Host Role

The host is a facilitator, not a player. The host should:

- Keep time.
- Enforce communication restrictions.
- Release evidence at the planned pace.
- Ask clarifying questions when discussion gets stuck.
- Prevent one player from being eliminated by a too-direct early clue.
- Keep the final reveal clear and fair.

## Opening Speech Pattern

Use plain language:

1. Name the incident and stakes.
2. Say every character was involved in the process.
3. Say every character may hold partial truth or hidden mistakes.
4. Define the goal: identify the primary cause or responsible party.
5. Clarify that players may explain, question, hide, misdirect, and defend themselves.
6. Explain voting and win condition.

## Evidence Release Pattern

Default:

- Opening: public stakes cards.
- First investigation: pressure or ambiguity clues.
- Midgame: connection and mechanism clues.
- Late game: decisive or near-decisive clues.

Do not release the strongest mechanism clue before players have formed competing theories.

## Steering Questions

Use these when discussion gets stuck:

- What directly created the incident, and what only made it worse?
- Which clue shows a mechanism, not just suspicious behavior?
- Who had enough information to prevent this?
- Which risk was known before the incident?
- What would have happened if this suspicious action did not occur?
- Is this a cause, a missed check, or an amplifier?

## Voting Rules

Simple version:

- Final vote identifies one primary responsible party.
- Correct vote: investigation team wins.
- Incorrect vote: culprit or hidden responsible party wins.

Layered version:

- Group win: correct primary cause.
- Personal win: each player keeps their hidden issue from being publicly confirmed.
- Training win: group can articulate system lessons in the debrief.

## Final Reveal Pattern

Use this structure:

1. Announce the vote result.
2. Announce the true primary cause.
3. Explain the incident chronologically.
4. Explain why the primary cause is primary.
5. Explain every other role’s responsibility without erasing the main answer.
6. Read the one-sentence truth.
7. Ask debrief questions.

## Debrief Questions

For team building:

- Where did communication fail?
- Which role had the hardest incentive conflict?
- What would have made the group solve it faster?
- Who used partial truth most effectively?

For training:

- What control or process would have prevented the incident?
- Which warning sign was ignored?
- What is the difference between root cause and contributing factor?
- How would this map to our real work?

For fun/social play:

- Which clue changed your mind?
- Who defended themselves best?
- What false theory was most convincing?

## Safety and Tone

Avoid making the game a real blame simulation for an actual team conflict. For corporate or classroom use:

- Use fictional names and companies.
- Avoid mirroring a recent painful incident too closely unless the user explicitly wants that and has facilitation support.
- Keep the debrief focused on systems, communication, and incentives, not shaming players.
